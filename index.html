About Me

I am a marine biologist investigating opportunities for scaling climate change mitigation abilities of marine and coastal ecosystems. I have spent considerable time quantifying ecosystem services of marine and aquatic ecosystems around the world with the Environmental Defense Fund, US Geological Survey, the Marine Mapping Lab at Deakin University, and Stanford Center for Ocean Solutions. My work bridges the gap between scientific research and creating practical, data-backed tools for resource managers and conservation practitioners.

As a marine spatial ecologist and blue carbon scientist, I bring extensive experience in geospatial modeling, intertidal field surveys, drone-based remote sensing, and research database management. I am also an FAA-certified drone pilot, enabling me to handle the full spectrum of project tasks, including data collection, reporting, and building unique communication tools.

Ecosystem Services Modeling: Blue Carbon

At the USGS Western Geographic Science Center, I modeled how sea level rise impacts ecosystem services provided by marshes and tidal forested wetlands of the Pacific Northwest. Focusing on soil carbon, I used GIS and soil models to project changes in carbon sequestration, informing the Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge's climate adaptation plans.

I also modeled blue carbon sequestration in marine ecosystems of Victoria, Australia as part of a joint postdoctoral position with Deakin University and UC Santa Cruz. The resulting maps are being used by the Victorian government to prioritize areas for habitat conservation and marine spatial planning.

Coastal Protection & Climate Adaptation

At Stanford's Center for Ocean Solutions, I modeled how California's coastal habitats store carbon and mitigate flooding driven by sea level rise. I mapped where people and infrastructure were vulnerable to flooding under different levels of climate change severity as part of the Realizing Environmental Innovations Program.

Our goal was to aid California's coastal jurisdictions in prioritizing natural habitats and climate adaptation in their Local Coastal Plan updates. I performed geospatial analyses and translated scientific information for an interdisciplinary team of policy experts, modelers, software developers, and biologists.

Sea Star Wasting Syndrome Research

At UC Santa Cruz, I earned my PhD studying intertidal ecology with the Raimondi-Carr Lab, investigating which environmental and anthropogenic factors are associated with the appearance of Sea Star Wasting Syndrome (SSWS).

Using GIS to integrate data from the North American Pacific Coast, I used disease observations from intertidal monitoring and citizen science reports to search for correlations between disease presence and environmental patterns — including above-average temperatures, population density, low-tide exposure times, and proximity to wastewater outfalls.

Beach Visitor Knowledge of Marine Reserves

I research the human aspects of conservation: what factors promote or hinder behavior for positive environmental outcomes. Tens of thousands of people visit California Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) each year, yet this paradoxically presents challenges for ensuring visitors are sensitive to their impacts.

We surveyed over 650 visitors at Santa Cruz County beaches and found that while visitors generally supported environmental etiquette, many did not know where the MPAs were located. Over half had heard of marine reserves, but less than one third knew whether they were standing inside one — highlighting a disconnect between awareness and specific location knowledge.
